There's a particular kind of meeting I've sat in dozens of times. A room full of smart, committed people, all laser-focused on a single question: *How do we do this better?* And almost nobody asks the prior question: *Should we be doing this at all?*

The nonprofit sector has a problem that the sector largely refuses to name. We've gotten extraordinarily good at measuring and improving operational efficiency — cost per beneficiary, overhead ratios, program delivery timelines — while systematically avoiding the harder, messier question of whether the programs we're running are actually producing the outcomes we care about.

> Efficiency at the wrong thing is not a virtue. It's a more elegant form of failure.

## The Efficiency Trap

Walk into any high-performing nonprofit and you'll find dashboards. Beautiful dashboards. Tracking volunteer hours, meals served, children enrolled, events completed. The data looks compelling. Donors love it. Boards approve budgets based on it. And yet, the fundamental question — does any of this produce lasting change? — often goes unexamined.

This is what I call the Efficiency Trap. The things we can easily measure become the things we optimize for, and the things we optimize for become the things we report as success.

## What "Wrong Problem" Actually Means

When I say nonprofit leaders are solving the wrong problem, I mean something specific. The problem being solved — operationally — often isn't the problem that the mission actually demands.

Here's a concrete example. I've worked with organizations whose core mission is breaking intergenerational poverty. Their programs focus on financial literacy training. Their reported success metric: 85% course completion rate.

What they rarely track: whether any participant's financial situation improved 12 months later. Not because they don't care. Because it's hard to measure, takes time, and donors rarely fund longitudinal evaluation.

## Why This Happens

Three forces conspire to keep leaders focused on the wrong problem:

**1. Donor incentives are misaligned.** Most funders reward short-cycle, easily-reportable outputs. Telling a donor you ran 47 workshops feels more accountable than tracking a three-year income trajectory — even though the latter is far more valuable.

**2. Leadership accountability structures lag the mission.** Most nonprofit boards evaluate CEOs on fundraising and operational performance. Almost none rigorously ask "is the theory of change working?"

**3. Sunk cost dynamics are powerful.** Once a program has been running for five years, shutting it down or restructuring it feels impossible. So we keep running it more efficiently.

## What Solving the Right Problem Looks Like

The organizations I've seen do this well share three practices:

They invest in **outcome measurement that's honest about the lag.** Real outcomes take time. They build this into their planning and donor conversations — even when it's uncomfortable.

They practice **active theory of change interrogation.** At least once a year, they ask: what has to be true for our model to produce the outcomes we claim? This isn't a five-minute agenda item. It's a two-day retreat.

They **create structural permission to kill programs.** When a program isn't working, there has to be a mechanism — cultural and operational — that allows you to say so out loud and act on it.

## The Implication for Leadership

The most important work a nonprofit leader does isn't optimizing delivery. It's maintaining clarity about what outcome they actually exist to produce — and building the organizational courage to confront evidence when the model isn't producing it.

The sector has enough efficiency. What it lacks is the willingness to ask, plainly and repeatedly: *Is what we're doing actually working?*

That question is uncomfortable. Which is precisely why so few people ask it.
